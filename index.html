Australia Beyond the Canvas

From gallery walls to grand concert halls.

A three-act data portrait of where Australia's love for the arts lives, who pursues it as a career, and what keeps it going.

Act I

Venues and Spaces

Australia is home to 589 art galleries and 1,047 theatres, the majority clustered along the eastern seaboard in a thick seam running from Brisbane down to Melbourne.

Not all of these venues are alike. Some are major institutions such as the National Gallery of Victoria and the Sydney Opera House, while others are small, independent spaces serving local communities. Where they sit closely tracks where Australians live, with the densest clusters in the major cities.

Australia's largest arts organisations operate on vastly different scales, from state galleries to national performing arts companies. Together, however, they reveal a common trend: revenues increased steadily after 2022, driven largely by major institutions such as the Art Gallery of New South Wales, National Gallery of Victoria and the Sydney Opera House. Only years with complete reporting are included.

Raw numbers follow population. The great concentrations of venues along the eastern seaboard reflect, more than anything, where most Australians live. But cultural access is not just about how many venues exist; it is about how evenly they are spread. Ask instead how many galleries serve each 100,000 residents, and the picture reshuffles entirely.

Chart 3  ·  Source

Galleries per 100,000 residents, by state

First place: ACT. No surprise that Australia's national capital leads the ranking. Home to the National Gallery of Australia and the National Portrait Gallery, the ACT fits a remarkable density of major collecting institutions into a territory of under 500,000 residents.

Second place: Victoria. Melbourne has long worn the badge of Australia's cultural capital, and the numbers back it up. Victoria's gallery network runs deep, from the iconic National Gallery of Victoria to a rich layer of commercial and community spaces spread across the state.

Third place: Tasmania. Small in population, Tasmania punches well above its weight. A long tradition of regional arts investment, capped by the arrival of MONA in 2011, has given the island state a gallery culture that belies its size.

Queensland and Western Australia sit at the other end of the scale: vast states where gallery access remains almost entirely concentrated in capital cities.

Buildings, though, are only the stage. What fills them with meaning are the people who study, make and perform the work inside, and they turn out to be a far smaller crowd than these thousands of venues might suggest.

Act II

Education and Occupations

Creative work is a smaller part of the economy than its cultural footprint suggests. Just 4.1% of Australians work in creative roles as their primary occupation, and another 4.5% do so as a secondary one. Yet it is this modest workforce, not the buildings, that keeps the sector alive.

Chart 4  ·  Source

Enrolments by Field of Study

It's easily recognisable that creative arts is not as popular amongst students in Australia as other fields of study. In 2024, there were only 96,067 enrolments in creative arts courses across the country, compared to 1,723,111 enrolments in Management and Commerce. Despite this, it remains a vital sector.

Despite the relatively low number of enrolments in creative arts courses, there is still a significant workforce in creative roles in Australia.

Comparing employment count against salary, the most populous roles, Graphic Designers, Photographers, and Interior Designers, all earn at or below the average, a sign that most people in this industry choose their career out of passion rather than pay.

On the other hand, the less populous roles such as Art Directors, Painters, and Sculptors earn significantly above the average, suggesting that these roles may require more specialised skills or experience.

Act III

Funding and Spending

Passion may explain why people stay in the arts, but it does not pay for the work. The artists and studios still need their materials, and the venues still need to cover their costs. Between 2014 and the end of 2025, Australia spent $3.7 billion on art material imports, made $28.2 billion in gallery and museum revenue, and received $102.24 billion in federal funding for heritage and arts.

Chart 8  ·  Source

Global sources of Australia's art material imports, 2014–2025

Most of what fills Australia's studios comes from one quiet source: New Zealand supplies over half the paints and nearly half the textiles, year after year. Italy is the exception, anchoring the trade in clay and stone, while drawing supplies bounce between New Zealand and Vietnam from one year to the next. The rest of the world fills in the margins.

Revenue and profit of Australian art galleries & museums

Revenue has barely moved since 2013, holding between $1.9 and $2.7 billion. Yet profit nearly quadrupled in 2017, when margins leapt from 8.5% to 31.9%, not from growth but from cutting costs. The institutions kept more while the practitioners who fill them earned the same.

Chart 10  ·  Source

Government funding for heritage & arts, 1999–2032

Public money tells the other half of the story. Federal funding for heritage and arts has grown almost without interruption since 1999, from $3.8 billion to a projected $12.9 billion by 2032, with only 2016 recording a cut. Spending on culture kept climbing year after year, but those gains stopped at the institution's door.

One box per year, read left to right. Each box fills to that year's funding level on a 0 to $15 billion scale. The thin bar at the left shows the year-on-year change: blue when funding rose, red when it fell on a 0 to 15% scale.

Three acts, one picture. Australia's arts sector is modest in scale but consistent in character: venues concentrated along the coast and unequally distributed per capita, practitioners who chose the work despite its wages, institutions that have quietly grown more profitable, and a government that has backed the sector with rising investment for over three decades.

It is not a glamorous economic story. But it is a durable one, and in the arts, durability is its own kind of achievement.
