Australia Beyond the Canvas

From gallery walls to grand concert halls.

A three-act data portrait of where Australia's love for the arts lives, who pursues it as a career, and what keeps it going.

Venues and Spaces

Before we ask who makes art or what it costs, we ask where it lives. Which walls hold it, which stages host it, which postcodes get to see it up close.

Act II

Education and Occupations

Infrastructure on its own does not make a creative industry. People do. This act follows them from the field they enrol in, to the university they pick, to the work they take up once they leave.

Charts 4 and 5

From study to wage — the creative workforce

Following enrolments out into the workforce, two questions remain. How many people actually do each of these jobs, and what does the job pay? The answers do not line up. Graphic Designer, Interior Designer, and Photographer dominate by headcount, but their pay sits near the middle of the pack. The best-paid roles, Artistic Director and the visual arts professions, employ only a few hundred people each. A creative career splits early: the common roles are steady but modest, the lucrative ones are rare and senior.

Employment count and salary deviation are shown as different chart types because they answer different questions — one about scale, the other about relative pay.

Act III

Funding and Spending

Behind every painting on a gallery wall and every set on a theatre stage is a supply chain. Having seen where art happens and who makes it, the closing act traces what keeps it running: the paints, pencils, clay, stone, and textiles that Australia buys from abroad to keep its studios open.

Chart 6

Global sources of Australia's art material imports, 2014–2025

Different materials, different maps. Chinese paints, Italian clay and stone, New Zealand drawing supplies, textiles spread across Asia. Dominance shifts year to year.
